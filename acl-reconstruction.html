Sports Knee Surgery

ACL Reconstruction

Anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction — restoring knee stability for active patients

ACL rupture is one of the most common and significant sports knee injuries. Without treatment, a torn ACL causes ongoing knee instability, increasing the risk of further joint damage. Mr Brett Bairstow performs ACL reconstruction using hamstring or patellar tendon grafts, with a focus on restoring full stability and enabling a safe return to sport.

About ACL Rupture

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) is one of the key stabilising ligaments of the knee. It is commonly torn during sports involving pivoting, jumping, or sudden deceleration. Complete tears rarely heal without surgery, particularly in younger, active patients.

Who Needs Surgery?

  • Active patients wishing to return to sport
  • Young patients with complete ACL tears
  • Patients with ongoing instability after physiotherapy
  • Those with combined meniscal or cartilage injuries requiring surgical treatment

The Reconstruction Procedure

ACL reconstruction is performed arthroscopically under general anaesthetic. A tendon graft (typically hamstring or patellar tendon) is harvested and passed through bone tunnels drilled in the femur and tibia, recreating the natural course of the ACL. The graft is secured with fixation devices and gradually integrates with the bone over several months.

Most patients are able to return to sport within 9–12 months with appropriate physiotherapy and rehabilitation.

What to Expect

01

Surgery

ACL reconstruction is performed arthroscopically. Most patients are admitted as a day case or stay one night in hospital at Mount Private Hospital.

02

Recovery — Week 1

Focus is on pain management, icing, elevation, and early movement. You will be walking with crutches and attending physiotherapy early in the recovery process.

03

Rehabilitation

A structured physiotherapy programme is essential. Rehabilitation progresses through stages of strength, balance, and sport-specific training over 9–12 months.

04

Return to Sport

Return to sport decisions are based on functional testing criteria — not time alone. This evidence-based approach reduces the risk of re-rupture.
