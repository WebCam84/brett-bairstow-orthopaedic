Hip Surgery

Hip Replacement Surgery

Expert total hip arthroplasty at Mount Private Hospital, Perth

Total hip replacement (total hip arthroplasty) is one of the most successful surgical procedures in medicine. Mr Brett Bairstow performs hip replacement surgery at Mount Private Hospital, bringing fellowship-trained expertise in arthroplasty from SportsMed South Australia and the Royal Bournemouth Hospital, United Kingdom.

What is Hip Replacement?

Hip replacement surgery involves removing the damaged surfaces of the hip joint and replacing them with artificial components. The femoral stem is inserted into the thigh bone (femur), and an acetabular cup is fixed into the pelvis. Together, these components recreate the natural ball-and-socket movement of the hip.

Who Needs Hip Replacement?

  • Symptomatic hip osteoarthritis that has not responded to conservative treatment
  • Avascular necrosis of the femoral head
  • Inflammatory arthritis (rheumatoid, psoriatic) with significant hip involvement
  • Post-traumatic hip arthritis
  • Significant hip pain limiting walking, sleep and quality of life

What to Expect

01

Pre-operative Planning

Mr Bairstow uses detailed templating and simulation software to plan implant sizing and positioning before surgery. This meticulous preparation is a hallmark of his practice.

02

Surgery

Performed under general or spinal anaesthetic at Mount Private Hospital. The procedure typically takes 1.5–2 hours. Modern minimally-invasive techniques are used where appropriate.

03

Hospital Stay

Most patients stay 2–4 days. Physiotherapy begins on the day of surgery or the following day, with walking aids provided to support your early recovery.

04

Recovery

Most patients return to light activities within 6 weeks and experience significant pain relief. Full recovery and return to sport typically takes 3–6 months.

Special Interest

Pre-operative Planning & Simulation

Mr Bairstow has a particular interest in preoperative planning for joint replacements — using advanced digital templating and simulation to optimise implant selection and alignment before entering the operating theatre.

This approach reduces intraoperative uncertainty, minimises the risk of leg length discrepancy, and contributes to improved long-term implant performance. It reflects Mr Bairstow's commitment to precision and evidence-based surgical practice.
